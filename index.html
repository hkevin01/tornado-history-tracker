Least Dangerous Fewest tornado tracks in the 30-yr record. Often near terrain barriers (ridgelines, plateau edges) or the outer study area. EF0–EF1 events are still possible; injuries are historically rare in these cells.
Low Below-average exposure. Some tornado activity recorded nearby over 30 years. EF1–EF2 paths have occasionally passed through. Minor structural damage and injuries are uncommon but plausible.
Moderate Near the regional average. EF1–EF3 events documented within or adjacent to these cells. Injury risk is real during strong outbreak days; property damage is possible.
High Above-average tornado corridor. Multiple significant EF2–EF4 tracks cross this area over 30 years. Elevated history of property damage, injuries, and fatalities.
Most Dangerous Located inside documented tornado super-corridors — including the April 27, 2011 EF5 track. Highest cumulative risk score. Serious structural damage, injuries, and fatalities are part of the historical record here.

EF0 · 65–85 mph — Light damage. Tree branches broken, minor roof and sign damage. Injuries are possible.
EF1 · 86–110 mph — Moderate damage. Roofs peeled, mobile homes overturned, vehicles pushed off road.
EF2 · 111–135 mph — Considerable damage. Roofs torn off, mobile homes destroyed, large trees snapped or uprooted.
EF3 · 136–165 mph — Severe. Exterior walls collapse, trains overturned, most trees uprooted. Fatalities probable.
EF4 · 166–200 mph — Devastating. Well-built homes leveled, cars thrown significant distances. Fatalities likely.
EF5 · 201+ mph — Catastrophic. Strong-frame structures swept off foundations. Mass casualties. An EF5 struck near Huntsville on April 27, 2011.
