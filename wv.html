Least Dangerous Fewest tornado tracks over 30 years. Usually corresponds to high-elevation plateau terrain, deep gorges, or areas where complex topography consistently disrupts wind shear.
Low Below-average exposure for the WV study area. Some tornado activity documented nearby. EF0–EF1 events are uncommon but historically possible.
Moderate Near the regional average for WV. EF1–EF2 events have occurred nearby. These zones are often lower-valley terrain in river corridors.
High Above-average exposure relative to WV standards. Multiple documented events in EF1–EF3 range. Usually associated with valleys that channel storms from the west.
Most Dangerous Highest relative risk in the WV study area. Note: “Most Dangerous” in WV is still substantially safer than “Moderate” in Huntsville, AL. Risk levels here are relative to the Appalachian region, not Dixie Alley.

EF0EF1EF2EF3EF4EF5
EF0 · 65–85 mph — Light damage. Tree branches, minor roof damage. Most WV tornadoes fall here.
EF1 · 86–110 mph — Moderate. Roofs peeled, mobile homes overturned. Documented in both WV study areas.
EF2 · 111–135 mph — Considerable. Roofs torn off, large trees uprooted. Rare but recorded in WV.
EF3 · 136–165 mph — Severe. Walls collapse, fatalities probable. Very rare in mountainous WV.
EF4 · 166–200 mph — Devastating. No EF4+ events in these WV study areas during the 30-year record.
EF5 · 201+ mph — Catastrophic. No EF5 ever recorded in WV. Compare to the April 27, 2011 EF5 that struck near Huntsville, AL.
